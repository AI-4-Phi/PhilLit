# Literature Review Plan: The Value of Traditions

## Research Idea Summary

This literature review examines how philosophy (primarily political philosophy, but also social philosophy, social ontology, sociology, and political science) analyzes and explains why and when traditions are valuable. The focus is on political and cultural traditions specifically, addressing the normative question of what makes traditions valuable, when they are valuable versus not valuable, and different philosophical approaches to explaining their worth.

## Key Research Questions

1. What makes traditions valuable according to different philosophical frameworks?
2. Under what conditions are traditions valuable versus harmful or without value?
3. How do different theoretical traditions (conservative, liberal, communitarian, critical) explain the normative status of tradition?
4. What role does tradition play in political legitimacy, social cohesion, and epistemic justification?
5. How do philosophers distinguish between valuable traditions worth preserving and problematic traditions worth reforming or abandoning?

## Literature Review Domains

### Domain 1: Normative Foundations of Tradition's Value

**Focus**: Core philosophical accounts of what grounds tradition's value - intrinsic vs. instrumental value, the metaphysics of normative worth, and fundamental arguments for why traditions matter.

**Key Questions**:
- What is the nature of tradition's value (intrinsic, instrumental, constitutive)?
- How do different meta-ethical frameworks assess tradition's normative status?
- What role does temporality and historical continuity play in generating value?
- How is tradition's value related to concepts like authority, identity, and meaning?

## Coverage Rationale

These seven domains provide comprehensive coverage of the normative question of tradition's value by:

1. **Establishing foundations** (Domain 1) before examining specific theoretical approaches
2. **Covering the major positions** on tradition's value across the political spectrum (Domains 2-4: conservative, liberal, communitarian)
3. **Examining distinct axes of value** beyond politics (Domain 5: epistemic value)
4. **Focusing on political applications** specifically (Domain 6: legitimacy and democracy)
5. **Critically examining limitations** to provide balanced assessment (Domain 7: pathologies and harms)

This structure ensures we capture both arguments FOR tradition's value and arguments AGAINST it, as well as conditional accounts that specify when traditions are valuable versus when they are not.

## Expected Gaps

Preliminary thoughts on gaps the research could fill:

1. **Systematic comparison** across frameworks: Most literature treats traditions within single theoretical traditions rather than comparing conservative, liberal, and communitarian accounts head-to-head
2. **Conditions for value**: Few works explicitly theorize the conditions under which the same tradition might be valuable or harmful (context-dependence)
3. **Pluralism about value**: Limited work on whether different kinds of traditions have different kinds of value (epistemic vs. political vs. identity-constituting)
4. **Dynamic assessment**: How tradition's value changes over time as social conditions evolve
5. **Practical guidance**: Most work is highly theoretical; limited guidance for evaluating actual traditions in policy contexts
