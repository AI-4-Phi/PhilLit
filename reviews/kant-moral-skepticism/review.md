## Introduction

Two interpretive tendencies have long shaped the reception of Kant's practical philosophy. On one reading, the Groundwork for the Metaphysics of Morals is an attempt to silence the amoralist — the figure who asks "why be moral at all?" — by demonstrating that moral requirements are binding on any rational agent, regardless of her particular desires. On another reading, Kant's moral philosophy is better understood as an articulation of morality's internal structure for agents who are already committed to moral demands; the "why be moral?" question is, on this view, one Kant never seriously intended to answer. Neither reading, however, captures the full landscape of Kant's anti-skeptical project. The present review argues that Kant's practical writings are directed at a spectrum of distinct interlocutors, each of whom accepts moral discourse in some form but doubts one or more of its structural presuppositions. Recovering these interlocutors — and assessing the argumentative resources Kant deploys against each — requires attending simultaneously to the exegetical question of what the texts actually argue and to the systematic question of whether those arguments succeed.

The research orienting this review asks, accordingly: what specific skeptical interlocutors does Kant's moral philosophy actually target, and what resources does it deploy against them? Sinnott-Armstrong's (2006) taxonomy of moral skepticisms — distinguishing epistemic, practical, and ontological variants — provides a useful analytic scaffold, but Kant's own skeptical landscape has a different topology. Guyer (2003) has shown that the refutation of skepticism is a more central Kantian preoccupation than deflationary interpretations allow, while Forster (2010) has pressed the case that Kant's anti-skeptical strategies are structurally continuous across the theoretical and practical domains even as they deploy distinct resources. The question is whether Kant's moral philosophy contains something like a unified anti-skeptical project or whether it offers a series of ad hoc responses to different challenges.

The review proceeds in three parts, each tracking a distinct dimension of this project. The first part maps the skeptical landscape that Kant's Groundwork navigates: the eudaimonist who reduces moral demands to enlightened self-interest, the knave or sophist who uses rational argument to rationalize non-compliance, and the question of whether Section III of the Groundwork deploys a genuinely transcendental argument against these interlocutors. Ware (2021) has argued that this deductive strategy is eventually abandoned in favor of a different justificatory model in the second Critique — the Faktum der Vernunft, or fact of reason. The second part examines the "moral atheist" exemplified by Kant's appeal to Spinoza in the Critique of the Power of Judgment: a figure who accepts the moral law but denies the providential cosmic order that makes sustained moral commitment practically coherent. Against this interlocutor, Kant develops the postulates of practical reason as a response to what is essentially a form of practical-rational despair. The third part examines the Faktum doctrine and its critical reception — the debate over whether Kant's appeal to the consciousness of the moral law as a self-authenticating given provides a genuine anti-skeptical anchor or merely postpones the problem.

The review identifies an underexplored connection binding these three parts. The Faktum and the postulates of practical reason are structurally parallel responses to parallel forms of doubt: both are non-deductive anchors that decline to derive their authority from some more fundamental principle, and both are addressed to interlocutors who have, so to speak, penetrated the surface of ordinary moral consciousness and found something to doubt there. Carvalho (2025) has recently argued that Kant's constitutivism in the Groundwork is directed at "explanatory skepticism" — doubt about how moral requirements bind — rather than "normative skepticism" — doubt about whether they bind at all. This distinction helps sharpen what is at stake: Kant's moral philosophy may be more defensible than standard critiques allow, precisely because its targets are more specific than the generic amoralist who simply does not care about morality. Recovering those specific targets is the prerequisite for assessing whether Kant's strategies are adequate to them.

## 1. What Kind of Skepticism Does Kant's Moral Philosophy Target?

Any adequate account of Kant's moral philosophy as an anti-skeptical project must first specify what kind of skepticism is in view. The term "moral skepticism" covers structurally distinct positions that require different responses, and the failure to observe these distinctions has generated persistent confusions about what Kant's arguments are supposed to accomplish and against whom they succeed or fail.

### 1.1 The Taxonomy of Moral Skepticisms

The standard analytic taxonomy distinguishes at least three structurally independent forms of moral doubt. Sinnott-Armstrong (2006) differentiates epistemic moral skepticism — doubt about whether any moral beliefs can be justified — from practical moral skepticism — doubt about whether there is always sufficient reason to act morally — and from ontological nihilism — the outright denial that moral properties exist. A fourth position, Pyrrhonian suspension of judgment, cuts across these categories. What this taxonomy establishes is that "moral skepticism" is not a single opponent but a family of positions whose members can be combined or held independently.

The relevance of this taxonomy to Kant becomes apparent once one asks which of these positions Humean sentimentalism actually represents. Forster (2010) argues that Hume posed a specifically theoretical challenge to Kant: by denying that reason can generate synthetic a priori knowledge, Hume threatened the foundations of both theoretical and practical philosophy. But the Humean challenge to morality is not straightforwardly epistemic skepticism in Sinnott-Armstrong's sense. Hume does not deny that moral judgments can be justified; he denies that reason, independently of sentiment, could either generate or motivate them. This is a challenge to the rationalist picture of moral cognition and moral motivation, not an endorsement of Pyrrhonian suspension. Guyer (2003) traces how Kant's critical philosophy was shaped from its outset by the need to answer this challenge without reverting to either dogmatic rationalism or Humean empiricism.

The upshot for the Kantian context is that the primary form of skepticism Kant confronts is practical rather than epistemic. The question Kant addresses is not whether we can have knowledge of moral truths but whether practical reason, operating independently of inclination, can determine the will. This reorientation — from epistemological to practical skepticism — is essential to understanding what the categorical imperative and the Faktum der Vernunft are supposed to accomplish.

### 1.2 Kant's Historical Interlocutors — Eudaimonism, the Knave, and Humean Sentimentalism

Within the practical domain, scholarship has identified several distinct historical interlocutors whom Kant engages. The most revisionary contribution here is Sticker's (2020) argument that eudaimonism — not amoralism, and not even Humean sentimentalism at its most challenging — is Kant's primary opponent in the second Critique. On Sticker's reading, the Faktum der Vernunft is directed specifically against the eudaimonist claim that what is to be done is determined by what serves the agent's self-interest. The Faktum establishes that morality cannot be reduced to prudential reasoning, but it establishes no more: it does not by itself refute act-consequentialism or other non-eudaimonist rivals that share with Kant's ethics the commitment to a rational principle over self-interest. This narrowing of the Faktum's target has significant implications for what the second Critique can claim to achieve against various skeptical positions.

Walschots (2024) provides the fullest historical contextualization of this reading, documenting the specific predecessors and contemporaries — Garve, Wolff, Helvétius — whose positions Kant was engaging in writing the second Critique. The Helvétian strand is especially important: Blank (2019) traces how Helvétius's environmentalist psychology reduced apparent moral motivation to a disguised form of self-interest shaped by social incentives and the economy of esteem, posing a challenge to any account that claimed reason could motivate independently of the reward structures of social life.

A second interlocutor is what Kant calls the knave — the agent who accepts moral language while systematically using sophistical reasoning to evade its demands. Callanan (2019) argues that Kant's description of the "natural dialectic" in Groundwork Section I, in which ordinary moral agents can be led to undermine their own convictions through sophistry, targets not the outright amoralist but a corruption of practical reason's self-understanding from within. This form of moral failure is distinct from both eudaimonism and simple indifference to morality: it presupposes engagement with moral reasoning while perverting it. Guyer (2007) traces a dual response to these interlocutors: Kant's naturalistic strand — grounding moral demands in the nature of rational agency — responds to the Humean sentimentalist's motivational challenge, while the transcendental strand — invoking pure practical reason — responds to the deeper challenge of how reason can bind finite beings regardless of their contingent inclinations.

The full amoralist — the agent who simply does not care about morality — occupies a more peripheral position in Kant's practical philosophy than is often assumed. Tenenbaum (2021) shows that, for Kant, full amoralism is psychologically incoherent: all rational action is pursued under some guise of the good, including immoral action, which involves a defective practical self-conception rather than a wholesale rejection of practical reason. The more philosophically significant opponent is the partial amoralist — the agent who acknowledges the form of moral reasoning while subordinating its demands to self-interest — which is the eudaimonist the second Critique targets.

### 1.3 Is Kant's Justificatory Project Addressed to the Skeptic?

A central interpretive fork divides readings of Kant's moral philosophy on the question of whether its justificatory project is addressed to a skeptical outsider or to an agent already within the moral standpoint. Grenberg (2013) argues for the latter: the Groundwork articulates and defends the structure of common moral experience for agents who are already morally engaged, not for skeptics who need to be converted. On her phenomenological reading, the "deduction" in Groundwork III is not a proof aimed at demonstrating morality's authority to a doubter but an analysis of the conditions under which ordinary moral agents understand themselves as bound.

This reading has structural support from the work of Kleingeld and Willaschek (2019), who argue that the Moral Law, on Kant's account, is an a priori principle of pure practical reason that neither requires nor admits of grounding in anything more fundamental — not in autonomy, not in a deductive argument, and not in a transcendental argument from the presuppositions of agency. On this "no-grounding" reading, the absence of a successful deduction of the moral law in the Groundwork is not a failure but reflects the correct understanding that foundational principles of practical reason cannot be derived from outside themselves.

Against this, Bojanowski (2017) distinguishes two senses of justification operative in Kant: justification of the content of moral principles and justification of their authority over us. His analysis supports the conclusion that Kant's mature position, expressed through the Faktum, abandons the project of grounding authority in anything external to moral consciousness — which is not a concession to the skeptic but a recognition that the demand for such grounding misunderstands the structure of practical reason.

Carvalho (2025) offers a useful refinement of this picture by distinguishing normative skepticism — the challenge "why should I be moral at all?" — from explanatory skepticism — the challenge "how can categorical imperatives bind finite rational agents?" Carvalho argues that Kant's constitutivism in the Groundwork targets only the latter: it explains how the categorical form of obligation is possible given the nature of practical reason, without claiming to convert the agent who sincerely asks why she should comply. Ware (2021) provides a developmental synthesis: different works address different challenges, with the Groundwork targeting eudaimonism and the knave, the second Critique addressing motivational doubt, and the postulates of practical reason addressing a third, structurally distinct form of skepticism — cosmic doubt about whether sustained moral commitment makes sense in a universe that may be indifferent to virtue's worth.

This three-way distinction — the eudaimonist/knave as target of the categorical imperative and the Faktum, the motivational doubter as target of the second Critique's account of moral feeling, and the moral atheist as target of the postulates — organizes the debates covered in the remainder of this review. Whether Kant's moral philosophy is best understood as addressed to the already-committed agent or to the skeptical outsider determines what success conditions the project sets for itself, and no single answer fits all three skeptical targets simultaneously.

## 2. Groundwork III and the Limits of Transcendental Argumentation

### 2.1 The Reciprocity Thesis and Groundwork III's Deductive Strategy

The third section of the Groundwork undertakes what Kant describes as a "deduction" of the supreme principle of morality: a demonstration that the categorical imperative is genuinely binding on rational agents, not merely a chimera. The argumentative architecture rests on what Henry Allison has called the "reciprocity thesis" — the claim that freedom (in its positive sense as autonomy) and morality (as the categorical imperative) reciprocally entail one another (Allison 1986). On Allison's reconstruction, systematically developed in his 2011 commentary, Groundwork III deploys a structure the skeptic can be brought to accept: any agent who reflects on her practical self-conception must take herself to act under the idea of freedom, and this self-conception, via the reciprocity thesis, commits her to the moral law. The argument does not presuppose noumenal freedom as a metaphysical fact but only the practical necessity of conceiving oneself as free (Allison 2011).

This reconstruction is compelling as an exercise in rational reconstruction, but commentators have raised the question of whether it is Kant's own argument rather than a philosophically improved successor. Santo (2011) argues that while Allison's reading is internally coherent, it cannot be attributed to Kant, because Kant's own argument requires moving from the cognition of objects to a critique of pure practical reason — a move that faces difficulties Allison's standpoint-based version avoids rather than resolves. The structural difficulty is identified with precision by Ameriks (1981) and Hiller (2015): Kant's inference from the speculative spontaneity of reason to the agent's existence as a noumenal free will goes beyond anything the practical context licenses. Crucially, Hiller (2015) establishes that Kant himself came to recognize this: in the B-edition revision of the first Critique, he retracted exactly the inference on which Groundwork III relies, which explains why the second Critique abandons the deductive project in favor of the Faktum. Smajevic (2020) provides direct textual evidence for what she calls a "radical reversal" between the two texts: where the Groundwork attempts to derive morality from freedom, the CPrR reverses the order of dependence, deriving freedom from the moral law as Faktum. This is not a merely rhetorical difference but a change in methodological foundations driven by Kant's own dissatisfaction with his earlier strategy.

The result is that Groundwork III's anti-skeptical strategy, however reconstructed, faces a circularity that Kant himself diagnosed. Sussman (2008) characterizes this movement from "deduction to deed" as Kant's recognition that the moral law cannot be derived from anything external to practical engagement itself. What remains contested is whether this diagnosis vindicates the Faktum as a superior anti-skeptical resource or simply concedes the failure of the transcendental strategy. Ware (2017) argues that the discontinuity is overstated: Groundwork III already relies on a moral conception of the intelligible world that plays a role structurally analogous to the Faktum, such that the two texts share more argumentative architecture than the "great reversal" reading admits. This continuity reading has implications for how one assesses the Groundwork's response to the moral doubter — but even if Ware is right, it does not dissolve the circularity concern so much as relocate it.

### 2.2 Stroud's Objection and Its Practical Application

Whatever the correct diagnosis of Groundwork III's internal difficulties, a more general structural challenge confronts any practical transcendental argument of the kind Kant deploys. Barry Stroud's 1968 analysis established the canonical form of this challenge: transcendental arguments can at most show that we must believe some proposition p — that it is required by the structure of our experience or deliberation — but not that p is true. The "belief/truth gap" means that even a successful transcendental argument leaves the skeptic with a coherent position: she can grant that deliberating agents must take themselves to be bound by the moral law while denying that they are (Stroud 1968).

Stern (2017) applies this objection directly to practical transcendental arguments and arrives at a pessimistic verdict. He examines "retorsive" arguments that aim to convict the moral skeptic of self-contradiction — the strategy of showing that the skeptic's position is performatively inconsistent — and argues that such arguments face a dilemma: either they collapse into ordinary deductive arguments, in which case the transcendental apparatus drops out, or they make the skeptic's position harder to rebut rather than easier. Practical transcendental arguments do not obviously fare better than theoretical ones simply because anti-realism in ethics is less revisionary than anti-realism about the external world (Stern 2017). The Kantian cannot simply help herself to the thought that showing the practical necessity of a belief suffices to establish its truth, since that would be precisely the move the Stroudian skeptic refuses.

Saunders (2021) defends Groundwork III against standard versions of these objections through a standpoint-based reading: the deduction operates by showing that from within the deliberative standpoint, the categorical imperative is unavoidable, and this standpoint-relative necessity is all that Kant needs. On this reading, the noumenal inference that attracted Ameriks's and Hiller's criticisms is not essential to the argument. Yet even this sympathetic reconstruction faces the Stroudian residual: to say that the moral law is unavoidable from within the deliberative standpoint is to show that deliberating agents must take themselves to be bound — which is precisely what Stroud allows — without showing that they are bound when evaluated from outside that standpoint.

Schutz (2023) mounts a more ambitious challenge to Stroudian pessimism. He argues that the substitution objection itself requires a transcendental commitment to sustain the skeptical challenge, generating a dialectical dilemma for the Stroudian: the very move that generates the belief/truth gap presupposes commitments that restrict the scope of the challenge. On Schutz's positive account, world-directed transcendental arguments reveal which beliefs are constitutive of our standard of objectivity — in the manner of Wittgenstein's meter-bar — and cannot be coherently questioned from within the practices they underpin. Whether this rehabilitates practical transcendental arguments specifically, or only their theoretical counterparts, depends on whether practical deliberation has the same constitutive role for practical objectivity that Schutz assigns to belief-formation for theoretical objectivity.

The overall picture that emerges is that Groundwork III's failure is not merely a contingent textual matter but reflects a structural limitation of the transcendental strategy applied to the practical domain. The moral doubter who grants that she must take herself to be bound by the moral law while denying that she is has not been answered by showing the practical necessity of moral self-conception.

### 2.3 Neo-Kantian Reconstructions — Korsgaard and the Constructivist Response

The dominant neo-Kantian response to this structural impasse is Korsgaard's constructivism, which reframes what the Groundwork's practical argument was attempting to establish. Korsgaard's 1986 paper distinguishes two forms of skepticism about practical reason: content skepticism, which doubts whether rational considerations apply to deliberation, and motivational skepticism, which doubts whether reason can motivate independently of desire. Her diagnosis is that external skepticism — the demand for justification of morality from a standpoint entirely outside the deliberative perspective — is misguided. The structure of the practical standpoint is not answerable to a further external demand for justification; anyone who seriously deliberates is already committed to the authority of reasons (Korsgaard 1986). The Sources of Normativity develops this into a systematic account: reflecting on the structure of agency — the "reflective endorsement" procedure — establishes that valuing one's own humanity commits one to valuing humanity as such, and this is presented as a transcendental argument from an undeniable feature of deliberative agency (Korsgaard 1996).

Korsgaard's most recent statement of this position (Korsgaard 2024) integrates the Groundwork and CPrR approaches, arguing that both categorical and hypothetical imperatives arise from the nature of action as rational causality, so that conformity to both is constitutive of action. On this reading, Groundwork III's claim that we must act under the idea of freedom suffices to show that we are bound by the imperatives constitutively, dissolving the belief/truth gap by making practical self-conception not merely a belief but a constitutive feature of agency itself.

Stern's (2017) assessment, however, is that this constructivist move either presupposes a prior moral commitment to the authority of the deliberative standpoint, in which case the argument is question-begging against the external skeptic, or it makes the skeptic's position harder to rebut rather than easier — since the skeptic can deny that the deliberative standpoint carries the authority Korsgaard assigns it. Sussman (2008) reaches a convergent conclusion from the internal side: if the moral law's authority can only be manifest through practical engagement rather than derived from anything external to it, the result is not a transcendental argument addressing the doubter on her own terms but a description of what morally committed agents find themselves conscious of. The parallel to Kant's own Faktum is deliberate: both acknowledge, in different idioms, that the deliberative standpoint cannot be vindicated from outside itself without circularity.

This structural problem recurs, as Ware's (2017) continuity reading itself suggests, precisely because any argument that aims to show the moral skeptic that she is already committed to the moral law through her agency must either presuppose that she is an agent in the morally relevant sense — which is contested — or operate with so thin a conception of agency that the moral content cannot be derived from it. The Faktum, as the section's conclusion anticipates, represents Kant's recognition that the anti-skeptical work cannot be done by a deduction at all, and its significance — whether it is a philosophically defensible alternative or a concession to the limits of practical reason — remains the central open question for interpreting Kant's mature moral philosophy.

## 3. The Moral Atheist, Spinoza, and Practical-Rational Despair

### 3.1 Spinoza as Moral Atheist — The Figure and Its Function

In the Critique of the Power of Judgment (KU 5:452), Kant introduces the figure of a righteous man who reveres the moral law but denies that any providential God governs the world. The example is transparently Spinozist: a morally serious agent who accepts unconditional moral demands while holding that nature is indifferent to virtue and vice. This passage has attracted growing attention because it identifies a third form of moral skepticism distinct from both the eudaimonist, who reduces moral obligation to prudential self-interest, and the practical amoralist, who simply refuses to take the moral law as authoritative. The moral atheist grants everything the eudaimonist and the amoralist deny — the categorical bindingness of morality, the non-reducibility of duty to happiness — and yet faces a practical-rational crisis that neither of those figures confronts.

Van Impe (2011) reconstructs the logical structure of what he calls Kant's "moral despair argument" against atheism. The argument does not claim that the atheist lacks moral motivation or moral knowledge; it claims that the atheist's position is practically self-undermining. If the natural world is permanently and systematically indifferent to moral worth — if virtue yields no proportional reward even in the long run — then a morally serious agent must eventually regard sustained commitment to morality as pointless. The agent does not conclude that the moral law is invalid, but she loses the practical capacity to act on it with the wholeheartedness that morality demands. Wood (1970) had named this the "Absurdum Practicum": without belief in a providential moral order, the morally serious agent becomes, in her own rational self-assessment, contemptible — someone who demands of herself what she simultaneously regards as pointless. Kerimov (2024) adds a crucial specification: what produces despair is not theoretical uncertainty about God's existence but the agent's confrontation with nature's concrete indifference, the experienced disproportion between moral effort and natural outcome that accumulates over a moral life.

Ellis (2018) develops the analysis further by distinguishing two kinds of moral faith that Kant deploys in response to this predicament. External faith consists in sincere assent to the theological postulates — God exists, the soul is immortal. Internal faith, by contrast, is a kind of self-contentment (Selbstzufriedenheit) grounded in the agent's own recognition of her moral integrity and her confidence in the moral order that her practical reason demands. Ellis argues that internal faith is the primary resolution to despair because it locates the agent's sense of moral coherence within the practical standpoint itself rather than in a theoretical commitment to providential theology. The implication for the project of identifying Kant's skeptical target is significant: if internal faith is primary, then the postulates are answering a question internal to practical reason — a question about the agent's own moral self-understanding — rather than supplementing moral theory with theological additions.

### 3.2 The Highest Good and the Structure of the Moral Argument

The response to the moral atheist takes the form of an argument from the highest good (summum bonum): practical reason necessarily demands the complete good — virtue crowned by proportional happiness — and this demand generates a practical necessity of believing in the conditions that make such an outcome possible. The logical structure of this argument differs from cosmological and ontological arguments in ways that have been clarified by recent scholarship.

Reath (1988) posed the question that structures much of the subsequent debate: does the highest good require divine providence, or can it be pursued through human moral and political effort? On the theological reading, God's role is indispensable — only a providential author of nature can guarantee the systematic proportionality of virtue and happiness that practical reason demands. On the secular reading, the highest good can be approximated through just social institutions and collective human moral progress, without postulating a providential God. Pasternack (2017) defends the theological reading against secular alternatives, arguing that Kant consistently held that human agency alone cannot achieve the modal requirements of the highest good — the systematic, not merely occasional, proportionality of virtue and happiness across all rational agents. Brown (2020) mediates between these positions with a theologico-political reading: neither God alone nor human political effort alone suffices; the highest good requires both divine governance and human institutional work. On this reading, what the moral atheist (Spinoza) lacks is not the entire project of moral and political striving but the theological dimension that guarantees its ultimate coherence at a cosmic scale.

Filieri (2023) traces the concept of the highest good back to the Canon of the first Critique, showing that it was originally introduced as an ideal unifying both speculative and practical reason, not merely a practical demand. This developmental context matters because it establishes that the highest good argument has a pre-second-Critique form in which the considerations driving it are not yet grounded in the Faktum der Vernunft. Pasternack (2011) then connects the postulates as they appear in the second Critique specifically to the Faktum: the postulates presuppose the unconditional validity of the moral law as already established by the Faktum rather than providing an independent argument for God's existence. This connection — that the highest good argument builds on the Faktum rather than replacing it — is philosophically crucial. It means that the moral argument is not a separate proof of God's existence but an extension of the same practical-rational logic that makes the moral law binding, now applied to the question of whether the moral law can be coherently pursued.

Kerimov (2025) makes this structural claim precise by identifying what he calls a "transcendental deduction of success" underlying both the deduction of nature's purposiveness in the third Critique and the deduction of the highest good in the second Critique. Both deductions proceed from the practical necessity of a goal — judging nature as purposive, achieving the complete good — to the conditions without which that goal cannot be coherently pursued. This is not an inference from theoretical features of the cosmos to God's existence; it is an inference from the practical necessity of an end to the rational necessity of believing in that end's realizability. If this structural reading is correct, the postulates are not an embarrassing appendage to Kant's ethics but a systematic extension of the same critical architecture that generates both the Faktum and the teleological judgment of the third Critique.

### 3.3 Moral Faith, Hope, and the Epistemic Status of the Postulates

The response to the moral atheist thus proceeds through practical-rational necessity rather than theoretical demonstration, but this raises an immediate question: what is the epistemic status of the commitment Kant demands? Chignell (2007) provides the indispensable framework for this question by mapping Kant's typology of propositional attitudes — Wissen (knowledge), Meinen (mere opinion), and Glauben (belief or faith) — and showing how each is grounded in a different sufficiency of the grounds for assent. Moral faith or Glaube is neither theoretically grounded (it cannot be, since the existence of God is theoretically undecidable) nor merely arbitrary: it is epistemically constrained by the requirement that the proposition believed be at least theoretically possible, and it is practically justified by its inseparable connection to the demands of the moral law. This makes moral faith a distinctive doxastic category — a rationally constrained attitude that the non-believer is not simply free to dismiss as irrational.

The internal debate within this framework runs between a doxastic reading (Glaube as a form of committed belief) and a hope-structured reading. Ferreira (2013) argues that the postulate's primary function is to sustain hope rather than to generate theoretical assertion, and that re-reading the postulate as a hope-structured practical commitment avoids the difficulties of treating Kant as recommending theoretical assent to claims that he elsewhere marks as theoretically undecidable. Stratmann (2023) sharpens the epistemic profile of hope by arguing that Kant holds a rationalist account on which hope is not a mere affective state or wish but a rationally constrained attitude requiring causal inference: to hope, an agent must take herself to have a cause for hope — rational grounds for expecting that the hoped-for outcome is possible. This distinguishes legitimate practical hope from wishful thinking and connects the postulates directly to the third Critique's "What may I hope?" as a genuinely practical-rational question.

Chignell (2022) mediates between Wood's (1970) coherence-based reading — which holds that belief in God is required for the formal coherence of moral commitment — and a moral-psychological reading that emphasizes the practical threat of demoralization. Chignell argues that the psychological formulation is more defensible: what the postulates respond to is not an abstract incoherence in the concept of moral commitment under atheism but a specific, experientially grounded risk of moral despair that accumulates over the course of a moral life. Di Giulio (2024) adds a further dimension by distinguishing two senses of "moral certainty" in Kant: the certainty embodied in rational faith in God and a future life, and the conscientiousness required in practical moral judgment. The connection between these two senses shows that moral faith is not a supplement to practical reasoning but a condition for the sustained moral judgment that a morally serious agent requires.

Zuckert (2018) introduces a significant complication by arguing that Kant's late essays — "The End of All Things" and "On the Miscarriage of All Philosophical Trials in Theodicy" — introduce moral counterarguments to the postulates themselves. Belief in God and immortality may generate fanaticism (projecting moral certainty onto contingent historical events) and fatalism (treating moral outcomes as the inevitable result of providential design), both of which undermine the autonomous moral agency that the postulates were supposed to support. Zuckert concludes that Kant's considered position treats God and immortality as regulative ideals of hope rather than endorsed beliefs — a reading that, if correct, makes the postulates less a response to the moral atheist than an acknowledgment that both the atheist's despair and the theist's certainty are forms of practical-rational failure.

The debate over Glaube, hope, and regulative ideal ultimately determines whether Kant's response to the moral atheist is genuinely practical-rational or a form of motivated reasoning — which would expose it to a further skeptical challenge. If the postulates are, as Zuckert suggests, regulative ideals rather than commitments, then they may address the psychological threat of despair without fully answering the moral atheist's structural objection. The project's contribution lies in connecting this debate to the Faktum: if, as Pasternack (2011) and Kerimov (2025) argue, the postulates presuppose and extend the Faktum rather than standing independently, then the moral atheist's practical-rational crisis is not an isolated problem about theology but a question about whether the unconditional demands of practical reason can sustain themselves in the face of nature's indifference — a question that cuts to the heart of Kant's entire practical philosophy.

## 4. The Faktum der Vernunft — Non-Deductive Anchor or Circular Concession?

The Faktum der Vernunft is the pivot of Kant's mature practical philosophy. Introduced in the Analytic of the second Critique, it holds that consciousness of the moral law is a datum requiring no deductive justification prior to itself — a fact, not a conclusion. But what kind of fact? And what work can it do against a moral skeptic? The interpretive literature has produced four competing accounts of the Faktum's logical status, a sharp debate about whether it replaces or reconceives the Groundwork's deductive project, and a persistent worry about circularity. These debates are not merely exegetical: each interpretive option carries different implications for how far Kant's anti-skeptical response can reach.

### 4.1: What Is the Faktum? Four Interpretations

The first and most basic question is what exactly Kant means by calling consciousness of the moral law a "fact of reason." The interpretive options carve out a logical space from the purely experiential to the purely formal.

Ware's experimental-method reading treats the Faktum as a witnessed, reproducible datum in the manner of British experimentalist natural philosophy (Ware 2014; Ware 2023). On this account, calling something a "fact" is not to call it a brute assertion but to invoke its publicly available, witnessable character. Just as an experimental effect can be repeatedly observed by any suitably placed observer, consciousness of the moral law is available to any rational agent as an experiential phenomenon. The Faktum is not a philosophical deduction but a deliverable of moral experience — something reason encounters and produces simultaneously.

A contrasting interpretation is the axiomatic model proposed by Willert (2023). Drawing on Kant's own statement in the Jäsche-Logic that the reality of the moral law "is an axiom," Willert argues that the Faktum functions as a productive starting point: undeniable in the sense of being unavoidable for practical reason, and productive in that it generates further claims (including the practical reality of freedom). On this reading, the demand for a prior justification of the moral law misunderstands its status — just as asking for a proof of Euclid's axioms misunderstands the role of axioms in geometry.

A third interpretation, the genitivus auctoris or "deed" reading, holds that the genitive in "Faktum der Vernunft" should be understood as a genitive of authorship: the Faktum is not a fact about reason but reason's own product or act. Wolff (2009), despite the incomplete abstract record for this article, has been identified in the secondary literature as developing this reading by arguing that the term "Faktum" in Kant's pseudo-mathematical method in the second Critique's Analytic designates a defensive strategy — the moral law's "deduction" invalidates objections by showing that any challenge to it makes a transcendent use of reason. Sussman (2008) converges on a related point: the shift from "deduction" to "deed" in the second Critique treats the moral law as constitutive of what it is to engage in practical reasoning at all, rather than a conclusion derived from prior premises.

A fourth approach, represented by Reath's formal principles essay (Reath 2010) and developed in the work of Kleingeld (2010), focuses on the moral law's formal character as what gives the Faktum its specific status. On this reading, what makes consciousness of the moral law a "fact of reason" rather than an empirical psychological state is that it is the direct manifestation of pure rational form in practical consciousness: universality as the form of a law just is what pure practical reason delivers, and this formal character explains the Faktum's non-empirical, non-contingent status.

These four interpretations — experiential datum, axiomatic starting point, deed of reason, and formal expression — are not mutually exclusive in every detail, but they differ on what makes the Faktum epistemically legitimate and on what it can claim to establish against a moral doubter.

### 4.2: Does the Faktum Represent a Retreat from or Completion of Groundwork III?

A second dispute concerns whether the second Critique's Faktum doctrine marks a genuine philosophical retreat from the deductive ambitions of Groundwork III or a reconceptualization of the same project under more defensible terms.

Ameriks (1981) articulated what became the "radical reversal" diagnosis: Groundwork III's attempt to derive moral obligation from the rational agent's intelligible standpoint is circular, presupposing what it seeks to establish. The shift to the Faktum in the second Critique is motivated by recognition of this failure. Smajevic (2020) develops a parallel argument, contending that Kant changed his methodological assumptions between the two works by making awareness of the moral law the new epistemic basis — not a continuation of the earlier deductive project but a reversal in the order of dependence between morality and freedom. Guyer (2007) interprets the Faktum move in similar terms, reading it as an abandonment of any genuine attempt to ground the moral law at all, reducing Kant's mature practical philosophy to noting what committed moral agents find in their consciousness.

Sussman (2008) represents a middle position: the shift from deduction to deed is a reconceptualization, not a failure. The second Critique treats the moral law as constitutive of practical rationality, so the "deduction" the Groundwork sought turns out to be unnecessary — the law does not need to be derived because it is what practical reason already is. On this reading, the Faktum replaces the failed deductive strategy not by abandonment but by identifying a deeper point from which no further derivation is needed or possible. Bojanowski (2017), while acknowledging the genuine discontinuity, argues against both the failure narrative and the facile continuity reading: the Faktum in the second Critique establishes the objective validity of the moral law for finite rational beings like us, whereas the Groundwork's deduction addressed the metaphysical question of how freedom is possible at all — these are different projects, and Kant's shift reflects appropriate specialization rather than defeat. Ware (2017) offers a structurally parallel point from another direction: both Groundwork III and the second Critique rely on what he calls a moral conception of the intelligible world, suggesting that the apparent "great reversal" is overstated and that the two texts pursue more continuous argumentative work than standard readings assume.

Allison (1990) occupies a distinct position: his reciprocity thesis — that freedom and morality are reciprocally entailing — is designed to show that the Faktum is not a brute post-hoc assertion but the practical expression of the same freedom-morality relationship the Groundwork argued for theoretically. On this reading, the Faktum is the first-personal counterpart to the Groundwork's argument structure.

The debate matters for the present project's central question. If the shift is a reconceptualization (Sussman, Bojanowski, Ware), then the Faktum is a philosophically defensible anti-skeptical strategy — different from a deduction but not a mere capitulation to the skeptic's pressure. If it is a retreat (Ameriks, Guyer), then Kant's mature practical philosophy concedes that the moral doubter cannot be answered on neutral ground and simply describes what moral consciousness is like for agents who already accept the law's authority.

### 4.3: Is the Faktum Circular? Can It Respond to the Moral Doubter?

The most acute challenge facing the Faktum as an anti-skeptical resource is the circularity worry. If the Faktum identifies consciousness of the moral law as its own validation, it appears to presuppose precisely what the moral skeptic denies. Kim (2002) surveys three influential interpretations of the "hidden circle" Kant himself acknowledges in the Groundwork, arguing that none adequately resolves it, and proposes a Faktum-based reading — treating moral consciousness as a regressive starting point rather than a vicious circle — as the most defensible response. The move does not eliminate the circularity worry entirely; it transforms its character.

Kryluk (2017) asks whether the Faktum has transcendental argument structure: treating consciousness of moral obligation as the conditioned, and freedom as its sole condition, the Faktum could be read as moving from a given datum to its unique explanatory presupposition. Kryluk concludes that while this transcendental structure is present in Kant's text, the Faktum is not simply a transcendental argument, because it relies on the specific character of moral consciousness as something reason both produces and witnesses — a self-certifying deliverable of practical rationality, not a theoretical inference.

Ware (2015) addresses the motivational dimension of the circularity problem: the feeling of respect for the moral law is not merely an effect of recognizing the law's authority but constitutes part of our access to it, providing a non-inferential affective route into moral consciousness. This matters because it addresses the skeptic's worry that the Faktum is an intellectualist abstraction with no motivational purchase on finite agents. Ware (2021) pursues the systematic consequence of these points: the second Critique's experimental methodology does not beg the question against the skeptic because the datum — consciousness of moral obligation — is publicly available to any rational agent, just as experimental evidence in natural philosophy is available to any suitably positioned observer. The circularity charge holds only if one assumes that the datum is accessible only to the already-committed moral agent; the experimental reading denies this.

Zapero (2017) addresses the question of whether the second Critique succeeds as a response to moral skepticism directly, distinguishing the forms of skepticism Kant faces and assessing whether the Faktum functions as a genuine anti-skeptical argument or merely articulates what practical consciousness is like. His analysis reinforces a distinction central to this review: a response adequate to the "moral doubter" — the agent who has not yet committed to the moral standpoint — differs from a response adequate to the "moral atheist" who denies the postulates while accepting the law's authority. The Faktum addresses the latter more naturally than the former.

This asymmetry reveals the Faktum's structural limit as an anti-skeptical resource. Addressed to the already-committed agent, the circularity worry dissolves but the response does not reach the outright amoralist. Addressed to any rational agent, as Ware's experimental reading proposes, circularity threatens but may be circumvented — at the cost of a demanding empirical claim about universal moral consciousness. The postulates share this structural feature: like the Faktum, they function as non-deductive practical anchors that respond to the demand for prior justification by appealing to what practical reason already finds within itself. Both refuse to justify morality from the outside, and both leave the sincere outright amoralist without a theoretical refutation. The review's central systematic claim is that this parallel is not a coincidence but reflects a unified structure in Kant's mature anti-skeptical strategy.

## 5. Critical Responses and Contemporary Reckonings

The preceding sections have traced Kant's anti-skeptical resources across his three main practical works: the reciprocity-based deduction of Groundwork III, the Faktum der Vernunft as a non-inferential anchor in the second Critique, and the postulates as a response to practical-rational despair. Contemporary philosophy has subjected each of these resources to sustained pressure. Two challenges stand out for the clarity with which they expose the limits of Kantian and neo-Kantian strategies: the shmagency objection to constitutivism, and Williams's internalism about reasons. A third, the realist alternative, illuminates the comparative position Kant's own strategy occupies. Taken together, these challenges reveal that the success of Kant's project depends on a precise characterization of his target — and that Carvalho's (2025) distinction between normative and explanatory skepticism is the key to an adequate defense.

### 5.1 The Shmagency Objection and the Limits of Constitutivism

The constitutivism debate recapitulates, in contemporary idiom, the circularity problems diagnosed in Groundwork III. Korsgaard's self-constitution thesis holds that the categorical imperative expresses the law of rational self-governance: acting immorally is failing at the constitutive task of being an agent (Korsgaard 2009). Enoch (2006) presses the objection that has come to define the debate: even if there are constitutive norms of agency, one can ask why one should care about being an agent rather than a "shmagent" — a being with all of agency's benefits but none of its normative constraints. The question is not obviously incoherent, and constitutivism lacks the resources to answer it without circularity. Tiffany (2012) sharpens the challenge by insisting that the same conception of agency must be used to answer both the content question (what are the norms of agency?) and the normativity question (why are those norms binding?). If a minimal conception of agency suffices for inescapability, it is too thin to generate substantive moral content; if a richer conception generates content, its normativity remains in question.

The structural parallel with Groundwork III is clear: just as Kant's argument that acting under the idea of freedom commits one to the moral law faces the objection that the skeptic can refuse to identify with the free standpoint, so too can the shmagent refuse to identify with full agency. Kim (2002) observes that Kant himself acknowledged a "hidden circle" in the Groundwork's deduction, and his alternative proposal — grounding the argument in the Faktum of moral consciousness rather than in a prior metaphysical account of freedom — represents a recognition that the deductive strategy is exposed to precisely this vulnerability.

Carvalho (2025) provides the crucial interpretive bridge. He argues that Kant's constitutivism in the Groundwork is not aimed at the normative skeptic who asks "why should I be moral?" from outside the moral standpoint, but at the explanatory skeptic who asks how categorical imperatives can bind rational agents given the nature of practical reason. On this reading, Kant's constitutivism is immune to the shmagency challenge because it does not claim to convert the sincere amoralist; it explicates the structure of categorical obligation for those who find themselves as rational moral agents. Schafer (2019) reinforces this line by reading Kant as a "capacities-first" philosopher: the relevant constitutive norms are grounded in the specific rational capacities that human agents actually possess — understanding and reason — rather than in generic agency as such. This makes the shmagency challenge less penetrating for Kant than for neo-Kantian constitutivists who begin from a more abstract conception of agency and then derive moral requirements from it.

### 5.2 Williams's Internalism and the Radical Challenge

Williams (1981) articulates what is perhaps the most radical external challenge to Kantian ethics: genuine reasons for action must be grounded in the agent's existing subjective motivational set. There are no "external" reasons that bind an agent independently of what she in any sense wants. This internalism directly challenges the Kantian claim that the structure of rational agency as such generates agent-neutral moral requirements. If Williams is right, the Kantian appeal to the categorical form of the moral law either reduces to something an agent can always rationally reject, or it illegitimately posits a motivational source — pure practical reason — that is not actually operative in human psychology.

Korsgaard's response in *The Sources of Normativity* is the most worked-out neo-Kantian reply (Korsgaard 1996). Surveying four accounts of normativity's source, she defends the Kantian autonomy account: normativity springs from our own reflective endorsement of principles of action, which is itself a constitutive feature of rational agency. Williams's commentary in the same volume challenges whether this succeeds, pressing the question of whether the reflective structure of agency can generate genuinely binding requirements or merely requirements that appear binding from within the practical standpoint. In *Self-Constitution*, Korsgaard (2009) develops the response further, arguing that moral agency consists in unifying oneself into a coherent agent through rational self-governance, and that to act immorally is to fail at this constitutive task.

Williams's challenge, however, illuminates a limit that applies not only to neo-Kantian constitutivism but to Kant's own strategy. It is one thing to articulate the structure of practical reason as it operates for those who find themselves subject to moral demands — to show how the Faktum of moral consciousness is not a brute datum but an expression of rational self-governance. It is another to convert an agent whose subjective motivational set simply does not include moral concern, and who can consistently refuse to identify with the rational standpoint from which the moral law issues. The significance of Carvalho's (2025) distinction is precisely that Kant's own project is plausibly directed at the former task. The Faktum is addressed to those who already find themselves morally committed; it articulates the conditions under which that commitment is rationally intelligible, rather than attempting to derive it from materials the moral nihilist is bound to accept. The same limitation applies to Kant's postulates: they respond to the practical-rational crisis of the morally serious agent who confronts the indifference of nature, not to the agent who is indifferent to morality from the outset.

### 5.3 Realist Alternatives and the Comparative Verdict

The realist tradition — represented by Enoch (2011), Scanlon (2014), and Parfit (2011) — offers a structurally different response to the moral skeptic. Rather than grounding moral authority in the constitution of rational agency, realists hold that moral requirements derive from stance-independent normative facts. Enoch (2011) defends robust non-naturalism: there are perfectly general and objective moral truths irreducible to natural facts, and this is compatible with a broadly naturalist worldview. Scanlon (2014) defends a "relaxed realism" about reasons: facts about reasons are irreducibly normative and constitute a distinctive domain of reality, requiring neither implausible metaphysics nor reduction to natural facts. By avoiding constitutivism's circularity, these positions sidestep the shmagency challenge — the normative skeptic's demurral from agency does not threaten a view on which moral facts hold independently of any agent's capacities or commitments.

The comparative advantage comes at a cost. Street (2006) poses the Darwinian Dilemma: evolutionary pressures on evaluative attitudes either tracked stance-independent normative truths (scientifically implausible) or did not (leaving our evaluative attitudes unreliable), and neither horn is comfortable for the realist. Parfit (2011) acknowledges that while Kant was correct that moral requirements are categorical, his specific arguments for this were flawed, and the realist synthesis he defends faces its own epistemological questions. Rawls (1980) inaugurated the neo-Kantian constructivist tradition precisely as a third option between skepticism and realism's epistemological burdens, arguing that moral objectivity can be achieved through the constructive procedure of the original position without positing stance-independent moral facts.

The comparative verdict is that each family of positions carries structural costs that parallel Kant's own. Realism avoids constitutivism's circularity but inherits epistemological challenges that Street's dilemma makes vivid. Constructivism avoids those challenges but faces the shmagency problem and the Williams internalism objection. Kant's own strategy occupies a distinctive middle position: neither a realism that generates its own epistemological difficulties nor a constitutivism that claims to answer the normative skeptic from outside, but a practical-phenomenological account that articulates the structure of moral consciousness for rational agents who find themselves morally committed. This is neither a concession to the skeptic nor a refutation of skepticism from neutral ground; it is a description of what practical reason already is, addressed to those who stand within the moral standpoint and seek its rational structure.

The broader upshot is that the contemporary critical literature, rather than undermining Kant's project, sharpens its proper scope. The challenges from Williams and the shmagency literature reveal that no anti-skeptical strategy converts the convinced nihilist from outside the moral standpoint. Kant's achievement, on the reading that Carvalho (2025) and Schafer (2019) help articulate, is to have recognized this limit — and to have addressed a more tractable form of doubt: the doubt of the agent who accepts the form of moral obligation but seeks to understand how practical reason as such can generate categorical requirements, and the doubt of the moral agent who accepts the moral law but despairs of its cosmic coherence. These are the targets the Faktum and the postulates are respectively designed to address.

## Conclusion

This review has argued that Kant's practical philosophy is best understood as a multi-front anti-skeptical project, one that targets not a single doubter but three structurally distinct figures: the eudaimonist, who reduces moral motivation to self-love and thereby denies the categorical character of the moral law; the sophistical rationalizer, who corrupts moral reason from within by confusing inclination with duty; and the moral atheist, who accepts the demands of morality but doubts the coherence of a world in which those demands can be fulfilled. Each skeptical figure receives a different response. The categorical structure of the moral law, established through the analytic of the *Groundwork* and the second *Critique*, is the first line of reply. The Faktum der Vernunft — the practical law's self-announcing presence to moral consciousness — functions as the second, displacing the need for a prior theoretical deduction. The postulates of practical reason, by warranting belief in God, freedom, and immortality, address the third, constituting the moral standpoint's own account of the conditions under which moral striving is not in vain.

What has gone underexplored in the literature is the structural parallel between the Faktum and the postulates. Both function as non-deductive anchors: neither is derived from theoretical premises, and neither claims to convert the skeptic through external argument. Instead, each articulates what practical reason already commits us to, given that we find ourselves as moral agents. The Faktum registers the unconditional authority of the moral law as a practical given; the postulates register the further commitments that moral agency, when fully reflected upon, generates. Whether the Faktum is best understood as a transcendental argument (Kryluk 2017), an axiomatic starting point (Willert 2023), an experimental datum of practical reason (Ware 2014), or a performative deed in which reason enacts its own authority (Sussman 2008; Wolff 2009) remains genuinely contested — and each reading yields a different verdict on whether Kant's strategy can answer the moral doubter or merely describe the grammar of moral consciousness from within. The present project takes this interpretive contest seriously rather than resolving it prematurely.

The contributions of this research are threefold. First, it proposes a tripartite taxonomy of Kant's skeptical targets that clarifies the functional relationship among the *Groundwork*, the second *Critique*, and the second *Critique*'s dialectic — three works that are often read in isolation but which address structurally different challenges. Second, it offers a systematic account of the Faktum and the postulates as parallel non-deductive strategies, showing that what appears to be a shift in argumentative register between the analytic and the dialectic is in fact a coherent and unified response to the full range of moral skepticism Kant identifies. Third, it defends Carvalho's (2025) distinction between normative and explanatory skepticism as the key to understanding the limits Kant himself sets for his anti-skeptical project: Kant aims to vindicate the binding force of moral requirements, not to provide a causal explanation of why agents comply with them.

This third contribution shapes the project's engagement with contemporary debates. The shmagency objection (Enoch 2006), Williams's (1981) internalism about reasons, and Street's (2006) evolutionary debunking argument each pose challenges that no anti-skeptical strategy can answer by converting a convinced nihilist from outside the moral standpoint. Kant's achievement, properly construed, does not require this. It is to articulate a practical philosophy that explicates the coherence conditions of moral commitment for those who find themselves morally committed — one that answers the doubts of the eudaimonist and the moral atheist from within the practical standpoint itself, without pretending to compel assent from a position that antecedes that standpoint altogether.
