## Introduction

A recurrent argument in the justification of racial profiling in law enforcement runs as follows: observed crime statistics show that members of certain racial groups are disproportionately involved in the offences being policed; differential enforcement is therefore a rational and perhaps efficient response to a real disparity in underlying behaviour. This argument has a structural flaw that is easy to state but difficult to formalise: the statistics invoked to justify profiling are themselves products of the enforcement practices those statistics are used to justify. The circularity is not merely rhetorical. Williams (2010) identifies it directly in his critique of Risse and Zeckhauser (2004), noting that pro-profiling consequentialist reasoning relies on crime-rate data generated by racially biased policing practices, making the statistical justification self-referential. Harcourt (2004) develops the same point into what he calls the "ratchet effect": directing enforcement toward a profiled group generates more arrests in that group, which produces statistics that appear to confirm the group's elevated crime rate, which in turn justifies continued and intensified profiling. Both analyses are qualitative. Neither provides a formal account of the epistemic mechanism by which self-referential evidence drives beliefs, nor of the structural confound that makes the circularity invisible to agents inside the system.

This paper asks, under what conditions does profiling generate a self-reinforcing feedback loop in which Bayesian updating on biased samples drives beliefs further from the truth rather than toward it? The answer developed here is that three conditions jointly suffice. First, an underlying behaviour is driven by poverty rather than by group membership as such, but the targeted group contains a disproportionately high share of the poor — a structural fact established for the United States context by Sampson and Wilson (1995) and confirmed in subsequent work by Western and Pettit (2010). Second, an enforcement official samples the two groups at different rates while ignoring income as a variable, thereby generating aggregate hit rates that conflate the causal effect of poverty with a spurious group-level association. This is a precise instance of Simpson's paradox (Simpson 1951; Pearl 2009): the aggregate statistic reverses the within-stratum association because a common cause — poverty — has been omitted. Third, the official updates beliefs by standard Bayesian conditionalization on the aggregate hit rates. Because the evidence stream is endogenous to the official's own enforcement decisions, the normative justifications for conditionalization — which assume that evidence arrives independently of the agent's prior beliefs — do not apply. The result is a formally coherent updating process that converges on false discriminatory beliefs.

The paper integrates three bodies of literature that have not previously been combined in this way. The first is the normative and formal debate over profiling's epistemic credentials, running from Risse and Zeckhauser's (2004) defence of actuarial reasoning in law enforcement through Harcourt's feedback critique and Knox, Lowe, and Mummolo's (2020) formal identification-failure result, which proves that administrative records generated by selective enforcement are statistically non-identified for estimating racial bias. The second is the causal-statistical literature on Simpson's paradox, where Pearl's (2009) directed-acyclic-graph framework provides the diagnostic vocabulary for identifying which variables must be conditioned on and which must not — a vocabulary that directly applies to the profiling scenario, where poverty is a confounder rather than a collider. The third is the Bayesian epistemology literature on the limits of conditionalization, particularly work on what happens when evidence is endogenous to the agent's prior beliefs and actions. The economic literature on self-confirming discrimination (Coate and Loury 1993; Fudenberg and Levine 1993) establishes the game-theoretic scaffolding for why such beliefs can persist indefinitely without self-correction; the present project provides a Bayesian-epistemological rather than game-theoretic formalisation, and identifies Simpson's paradox as the specific confounding mechanism that these earlier models lack.

The review proceeds as follows. Section 1 establishes the normative stakes of the profiling debate and traces the formal genealogy of self-confirming discrimination from the statistical discrimination literature through the policing feedback literature. Section 2 develops the causal-statistical and Bayesian-epistemological machinery — Simpson's paradox and the limits of conditionalization under endogenous evidence — that the model requires. Section 3 grounds the model's structural premises in the empirical literature on poverty, race, and policing, and extends the analysis to algorithmic predictive policing systems where the feedback loop operates at scale with additional formal fairness constraints.

## Section 1: The Profiling Debate and the Formal Genealogy of Self-Confirming Discrimination

The pro-profiling position in both philosophy and economics rests on a single load-bearing epistemic premise: that observed crime-rate differentials between racial groups track real propensity differences and thus provide legitimate actuarial grounds for differential enforcement. The philosophical literature on profiling has contested this premise on normative grounds without fully dismantling it formally; the formal economic literature has modelled self-confirming discriminatory beliefs without applying the analysis to the policing context with sufficient precision. Both literatures converge on the insight that discriminatory enforcement is epistemically self-sustaining—but the specific mechanism by which enforcement-generated statistics produce the appearance of justification has not been formally identified. This section maps that convergence and locates the gap the research project fills.

### 1.1 The Epistemic Justification Debate

The contemporary philosophical debate over racial profiling is structured, in substantial part, as a response to Risse and Zeckhauser (2004), who argue that profiling is morally permissible—and in certain conditions morally required—when statistical evidence indicates higher crime-propensity rates among a profiled group. Their position is grounded in a consequentialist-actuarial framework: if group membership correlates with the behaviour being policed, directing enforcement toward the group increases detection efficiency and reduces overall crime costs. The key epistemic premise is that observed crime-rate differentials are accurate measures of underlying propensity differences. Schauer (2009) provides the most sustained philosophical defence of the reasoning structure Risse and Zeckhauser rely on, arguing that generalisation-based reasoning is epistemically unavoidable and that statistical profiling becomes impermissible only when the underlying generalisation is inaccurate or morally objectionable on other grounds.

This epistemic premise has been contested from multiple directions. Lever (2005) argues that Risse and Zeckhauser underestimate the epistemic uncertainty of the statistical base rates they invoke: crime statistics are products of prior enforcement decisions, meaning their evidential value for propensity claims is far lower than the actuarial model assumes. Lever (2007) extends this by grounding the anti-profiling argument in democratic equality: treating citizens as representatives of statistical categories rather than as individual agents with individual records of conduct violates the kind of equal standing that democratic membership requires. Williams (2010) develops the circularity objection most directly: the crime statistics on which the pro-profiling argument relies are themselves generated by racially biased policing practices, making the justification for profiling depend on data that profiling itself produces. This circularity is noted qualitatively but not formally analysed.

Harcourt (2004) offers the most thorough analytical critique of the economic literature on profiling, focusing on what he calls the "ratchet effect": directing enforcement toward a profiled group generates more arrests in that group, which appears to confirm the group's elevated crime rate, which justifies continued profiling. Crucially, Harcourt shows that the hit-rate test proposed by Knowles, Persico, and Todd (2001)—under which equal success rates across groups would indicate non-discriminatory enforcement—is defeated by this feedback mechanism, because equal hit rates can be achieved by discriminatory enforcement that suppresses criminal behaviour in the profiled group. Atenasio (2020) extends the empirical challenge, arguing that race is a comparatively weak predictor of the behaviours being policed once socioeconomic variables are controlled for, and that the apparent race-crime correlation is substantially an artefact of poverty concentration in profiled groups.

The pro-profiling position has been defended against these objections. Sesardic (2018) argues that critics of profiling must engage directly with the available empirical data on crime rates rather than dismissing those data on the grounds that they might be endogenous, and that a suitably cautious actuarial argument survives the circularity objection in most real-world enforcement contexts. Risse (2007), responding to Lever and Lippert-Rasmussen, concedes that race should rarely be a decisive factor in enforcement and that institutional safeguards are essential, but maintains that the conditional epistemic justification for profiling holds when the statistical evidence meets appropriate standards. The disagreement, then, turns ultimately on whether the observed statistics can serve as independent evidence of propensity—a question that the present research project addresses through formal Bayesian modelling.

### 1.2 The Normative Wrong of Profiling Beyond Accuracy

A substantial parallel literature argues that profiling would be wrongful even if its epistemic premise were sound. These arguments establish that the research project's formal critique of the accuracy claim does not exhaust the normative case against profiling but rather strengthens a set of independently grounded objections.

Hosein (2018) argues that racial profiling wrongs its targets by giving them a reasonable sense of inferior political status—that they are treated as second-class members of the political community whose equal standing is not respected. This wrong is constituted by the expressive and communicative content of the practice, not by its accuracy or inaccuracy. Mogensen (2017) develops a cumulative-injustice account: any individual profiling encounter might seem proportionate, but the aggregate burden placed on profiled individuals across their lives and across social settings becomes unjust against the background of existing racial disadvantage. This cumulative framing connects to the project's concern with feedback: the model shows that the aggregate effect of the feedback mechanism is to systematically entrench and worsen structural disadvantage, making the cumulative injustice substantially larger than the sum of individual incidents.

Bou-Habib (2011) provides the account in the philosophical literature that most directly anticipates the project's poverty-confounding mechanism. He argues that racial profiling is wrong not primarily as a freestanding violation but as an act that compounds background social injustice—specifically racial inequality in poverty and socioeconomic opportunity. Because race functions as a proxy for socioeconomic disadvantage in the enforcement context, profiling effectively punishes that disadvantage and converts it into additional criminal-justice burdens. The formal model in the research project shows exactly how this confounding produces a spurious race-crime correlation: poverty is a common cause of both the targeted behaviour and the disproportionate poverty concentration in the profiled group, and the official's failure to condition on poverty generates the appearance of a group-level propensity difference.

Eidelson (2015) locates the wrong of profiling in a failure of recognition: the profiler treats the individual as merely a member of a statistical category, denying the recognition of individual moral agency that grounds the presumption of innocence. DeAngelis (2014) develops this through the presumption-of-innocence framework directly, arguing that profiling imposes suspicion that cannot be individually grounded. Lippert-Rasmussen (2006) adds a communitarian dimension, arguing that profiling causes harm to the profiled community as a whole by conveying collective suspicion and eroding the social trust and information flows that accurate policing requires. Lippert-Rasmussen (2013) provides the systematic philosophical framework, distinguishing direct from indirect discrimination and arguing that the wrongness of statistical discrimination stems from how it unfairly disadvantages on protected grounds, regardless of whether the discriminator acts from animus.

Fricker's (2007) framework of epistemic injustice provides a further dimension: profiled individuals suffer testimonial injustice when their denials of guilt are discounted because of credibility deficits assigned on the basis of group membership. De Bruin (2025) extends this mechanism formally, introducing the concept of self-fulfilling testimonial injustice—credibility deficits that become true through the conditions they create. Drawing on the Coate-Loury investment-trap model, de Bruin shows how credibility deficits can become self-fulfillingly entrenched when those whose testimony is undervalued have reduced incentives to produce high-quality testimony. This bridges the epistemic-injustice literature and the formal economic models of statistical discrimination that the research project mobilises.

### 1.3 Formal Models of Self-Confirming Discrimination

The formal economic literature on statistical discrimination establishes the theoretical lineage within which the research project positions itself. This lineage begins with Phelps (1972) and Arrow (1973), who showed independently that profit-maximising employers with no taste for discrimination will nonetheless use group membership as a signal for unobserved individual productivity when group averages are believed to differ. Phelps models the employer's rational use of race as a prior: the noisier the individual test score, the more weight is placed on the group mean. Arrow introduces the equilibrium perspective, showing that employer beliefs about group investment levels interact with workers' investment decisions to produce self-sustaining discriminatory equilibria. The key insight Arrow introduces—that beliefs can be stable not because they are accurate but because they generate confirming behaviour—is the direct conceptual ancestor of the feedback mechanism the research project models in the policing context.

Coate and Loury (1993) formalise this insight in a game-theoretic model with two groups and employer beliefs about workers' propensity to invest in qualifications. Multiple Nash equilibria exist: in the discriminatory equilibrium, low employer expectations rationally induce minority workers to under-invest, which in turn confirms the low expectations. The discriminatory equilibrium persists precisely because agents' on-path experience provides no evidence against it—the employer never observes the investment minority workers would have made under different expectations. Fang and Moro (2010), surveying this literature, clarify that the Coate-Loury mechanism rests on a strategic complementarity between employer beliefs and worker investment rather than on any informational asymmetry, and that multiple equilibria are a robust feature of this class of model.

Fudenberg and Levine (1993) provide the formal game-theoretic machinery that explains the persistence of the discriminatory equilibrium. They define self-confirming equilibrium (SCE) as a strategy profile where each player's strategy is a best response to their beliefs, and beliefs are correct along the equilibrium path but may be wrong off it. Because off-path events never occur, players never receive data that contradicts their off-path beliefs. In the policing context, the profiling officer who never investigates the un-profiled group never learns that the un-profiled group has a similar base rate: the off-path belief that the un-profiled group is low-risk is never tested and thus persists indefinitely. Fudenberg and Levine (2006) further characterise which false beliefs survive rational learning, showing that "superstitions" at least two steps off the equilibrium path are never refuted by single deviations—the conditions that make discriminatory beliefs about un-policed populations structurally immune to correction are precisely those created by consistent profiling.

Esponda (2008) extends self-confirming equilibrium to agents who fail to correct for selection in their data. Officers who observe arrests only within the profiled group, and who behave as if the observed sample is representative, converge to a steady state in which beliefs are locally consistent with observed data yet globally wrong. This models the mechanism of the research project directly: the enforcement decision determines the sample, and agents who ignore this endogeneity will form stable false beliefs. Esponda and Pouzo (2016) generalise this to Berk-Nash equilibrium, covering agents with structurally misspecified models of their environment. An officer who attributes poverty-driven behaviour to race holds a misspecified structural model; under Berk-Nash, that officer converges to confident wrong beliefs because the misspecified race model fits the observed data better, from the officer's perspective, than the correct poverty model—particularly when poverty and race are correlated in the observed population. This gives formal support to the research project's Simpson's paradox argument: the misspecification is not random noise but a systematic bias produced by the correlation structure of the data-generating process.

In the policing context specifically, Harcourt (2007) provides the most sustained account of what he calls the actuarial ratchet: directing enforcement toward high-elasticity groups depresses criminal behaviour in those groups and shifts the crime burden to less-targeted groups, changing observed rates in ways that appear to justify the original profile. Knox, Lowe, and Mummolo (2020) formalise the identification problem that underlies the entire literature: if police racially discriminate at the initial contact stage, then all subsequent administrative data—arrests, use-of-force records, search outcomes—are contaminated by post-treatment selection. Traditional estimators applied to such data are statistically biased, and many causal quantities of interest are unidentified without strong and untestable assumptions. This proves formally that the observed enforcement statistics that appear to justify profiling are not independent measures of underlying behaviour but artifacts of enforcement intensity. The result applies directly to the hit-rate test: Knowles, Persico, and Todd's (2001) proposed benchmark for non-discrimination is rendered unidentified once the selection problem Knox et al. formalise is acknowledged. Gelman, Fagan, and Kiss (2007) illustrate the empirical stakes using multilevel regression on over 175,000 NYPD stop-and-frisk records, finding that stops of Black and Hispanic pedestrians exceed what would be predicted by precinct-level crime rates even after controlling for geographic heterogeneity—while facing the same identification challenge their statistical approach cannot fully resolve.

Merton (1948) provides the sociological archetype for this entire class of mechanisms: the self-fulfilling prophecy, in which a false belief that is acted upon causes the very outcome it predicts. His extension to racial discrimination—beliefs about group characteristics generate the social conditions that appear to confirm them—identifies the closed causal loop that prevents rational evidence-gathering from breaking the cycle. The formal economic literature operationalises this insight, and the research project's contribution is to extend it to policing by identifying the specific confounding mechanism (poverty as an omitted variable) and formalising it as a case of Simpson's paradox operating on a Bayesian updating process.

The research project thus intervenes at the intersection of three formally established results: Harcourt's ratchet effect (qualitative), Knox et al.'s identification failure (causal-inferential), and the Coate-Loury / Fudenberg-Levine / Esponda lineage (game-theoretic). What is absent from all three is a Bayesian-epistemological analysis that identifies the specific confounding structure—poverty as the omitted variable creating a Simpson's paradox—through which the profiling officer's belief system is driven away from truth despite formally valid updating. The normative literature, meanwhile, has established independent moral wrongs that this epistemic failure compounds. The pro-profiling position depends on an epistemic premise that formal models show to be structurally defeated; the normative literature establishes additional wrongs that the feedback mechanism intensifies; and the project's Bayesian model provides the missing formal link between them.

## Section 2: Simpson's Paradox and the Epistemology of Endogenous Evidence

The formal case against profiling's epistemic credentials rests on two interlocking mechanisms: a confounding structure that causes aggregate statistics to misrepresent within-group relationships, and a Bayesian updating framework whose normative justifications presuppose conditions that profiling systematically violates. This section establishes each mechanism in turn, then shows how they combine to produce a self-reinforcing feedback loop that drives beliefs away from the truth.

### 2.1 Simpson's Paradox -- Causal Structure and Variable Selection

Simpson's paradox names the reversal of a statistical association between two variables upon conditioning on a third. Simpson (1951) first provided a systematic mathematical treatment of the phenomenon in contingency tables, though Yule (1903) had earlier identified the general problem of associations changing sign when populations are combined or decomposed. Blyth (1972), who coined the label, connected the paradox to a normative dimension: the reversal violates Savage's Sure-Thing Principle, showing that agents who aggregate over a third variable may act against their own interests in every identifiable subpopulation. In the profiling context, this means an official's aggregate hit-rate statistic is structurally incapable of guiding accurate inferences about any subgroup's actual risk profile.

The philosophical question the paradox raises is which statistic -- aggregate or disaggregated -- is the epistemically and practically appropriate one. Three broad positions can be distinguished in the literature. The first, represented by Pearl (2009, 2014), resolves the question causally through directed acyclic graphs (DAGs): whether to condition on a third variable depends on its causal role. If the variable is a confounder -- a common cause of both the exposure and the outcome -- stratification is required; if it is a collider -- a common effect -- stratification introduces spurious correlation. Pearl (2014) argues that purely statistical criteria cannot adjudicate among these structures, and that the paradox endures precisely because reasoners resist importing causal information into statistical analysis. In the profiling scenario, poverty is a confounder: it causes both the targeted behaviour (via financial pressure and differential opportunity) and, through structural inequality, disproportionate membership in the profiled minority group. The official's failure to condition on poverty means the aggregate hit rate conflates the causal effect of group membership with the confounding influence of poverty. Hernan, Clayton, and Keiding (2011) demonstrate in an epidemiological context that the "paradox" dissolves once the causal structure is made explicit; their methodological template applies directly to the profiling model.

The second position, represented by Otte (1985) and Cartwright (1979), holds that the paradox exposes a fundamental limitation of probabilistic theories of causation. Otte argues that no purely probabilistic criterion adequately resolves the paradox without importing independent causal assumptions; Cartwright contends that causal laws cannot be read off population-level correlations alone, encapsulated in her dictum "no causes in, no causes out." The observed correlation between group membership and detected offending is consistent with multiple causal graphs -- including the one in which poverty drives both -- and the official's inference that group membership is causal requires prior causal information that the statistics alone do not supply. Eells (1991) develops the most detailed philosophical account within a probabilistic causation framework, arguing that the appropriate partition for evaluating whether one variable causes another consists of all factors causally relevant to the outcome independently of the candidate cause. Applied to profiling, the causally homogeneous partition is defined by income stratum, and within that partition the group-level association vanishes.

The third position, advanced by Armistead (2014), dissents from Pearl's purely causal resolution. Armistead argues that noncausal third variables can carry critical information about study design, hidden confounders, and data reliability, and that any variable producing a Simpson's reversal warrants investigation regardless of its causal status. In the profiling context, this position is not a defence of aggregate statistics but a strengthening of the case against them: even if the official is uncertain whether income is causally prior to group membership, the empirical reversal upon stratification by income is itself a reason to suspend judgment on the aggregate rate. Fitelson (2017) offers a further epistemic-level diagnosis: the paradox exploits an ambiguity between suppositional and conjunctive readings of confirmation. A decision-maker can coherently hold -- under one reading -- that within each income stratum group membership does not predict the targeted behaviour, while also believing -- under a different reading -- that it predicts behaviour in the aggregate. This models the cognitive structure of biased statistical reasoning without requiring bad faith.

A more recent complication is introduced by Dong, Cai, and Zhao (2024), who argue that Pearl's confounding-based analysis does not fully resolve the paradox because reversal can arise from causal structures involving mediation, selection, and inter-unit causation, not only confounding. In the profiling context, differential enforcement intensity generates a second-stage selection effect -- increased scrutiny of the profiled group raises the rate of detected offences -- that may involve a collider or mediator rather than a simple confounder. This distinction carries policy consequences: the appropriate analytical response differs depending on whether the reversal arises from omitting a confound or from conditioning on a collider.

The canonical real-world illustration is the Berkeley admissions case (Bickel, Hammel, and O'Connell 1975). Aggregate data showed apparent discrimination against women applicants; disaggregation by department reversed or eliminated the bias, because women disproportionately applied to more competitive departments. The structural analogy to profiling is exact: an aggregate disparity in hit rates by group is explained by a confounding structural variable (department selectivity / income stratum) rather than by direct group-level propensity. Good and Mittal (1987) supply the algebraic conditions for when such reversal is guaranteed: amalgamation of two contingency tables reverses the association when subgroup membership is correlated with stratum membership -- precisely the condition that obtains when the profiled minority group is disproportionately poor.

### 2.2 Bayesian Conditionalization and Its Limits Under Endogenous Evidence

The formal epistemological question is what happens when a Bayesian agent updates on statistics generated by the confounding structure described above. Bayesian conditionalization -- the norm that upon receiving evidence E, an agent should set their new credence in hypothesis H to their prior conditional credence P(H|E) -- is the dominant framework for rational belief revision. Its normative credentials rest on three independent lines of argument: Dutch book arguments (Skyrms 1987), accuracy-dominance arguments (Briggs and Pettigrew 2018), and expected-accuracy arguments (Greaves and Wallace 2006). Each of these justifications, however, presupposes that the evidence stream is exogenous -- determined independently of the agent's prior beliefs and actions. When this presupposition fails, the justifications break down in structurally analogous ways.

Skyrms (1987) grounds conditionalization in dynamic coherence: an agent who plans to violate conditionalization is diachronically Dutch-bookable. But this coherence argument does not guarantee that beliefs converge to truth; it ensures only that they form an internally consistent probability function. Greaves and Wallace (2006) provide an accuracy-based justification: among all possible updating procedures, conditionalization maximizes expected epistemic utility. Briggs and Pettigrew (2018) strengthen this with a dominance argument that does not require advance knowledge of the evidence partition. These accuracy-based arguments assume that the worlds over which accuracy is assessed are determined independently of the agent's updating behavior. When an agent's enforcement decisions generate the evidence, this independence fails: the worlds the agent actually encounters are not a neutral sample of the possibility space but a biased selection shaped by prior beliefs. The strongest accuracy-based defence of conditionalization thus dissolves precisely when evidence is endogenous.

Vassend (2026) provides the most recent philosophical analysis of this failure mode. Distinguishing "internal" rationality -- coherence with one's own probability function -- from "ecological" rationality -- convergence to truth given the actual environment -- Vassend demonstrates that conditionalization can be ecologically suboptimal in common real-world scenarios even for computationally unbounded ideal agents. When an agent's model of the world is misspecified or evidence arrives from non-representative samples, ecological rationality diverges from internal rationality. The profiling agent is internally rational: they observe hit rates and conditionalize. But they are ecologically irrational: the evidence stream is shaped by their own enforcement decisions, and their model omits the confounding role of poverty. Gallow (2019) develops a complementary diagnosis from an externalist perspective. If externalism about evidence is correct, then conditionalization becomes self-defeating: an externalist Bayesian who conditionalizes on biased evidence without recognizing its non-representative character risks "deliberate self-delusion" -- repeatedly raising their credence in a false proposition as evidence accumulates. A profiling agent who treats their biased sample as ordinary evidence, without awareness that scrutinized individuals are a non-representative subset, faces exactly this pathology.

The zero-raising problem identified by Raidl (2020) introduces a further mechanism of entrenchment. Orthodox Bayesianism holds that once an agent assigns zero prior probability to a hypothesis, no evidence can revise this upward. If a profiling agent assigns near-zero probability to the hypothesis that the profiled group has low base-rate criminality, conditional on their prior enforcement patterns, the updating norm itself blocks revision. Raidl proposes epsilon-conditionalization as a minimal repair: mixing the prior with a small weight on neglected possibilities before conditionalizing. This points toward the structural condition for breaking the feedback loop -- only deliberate injection of epistemic humility can create room for revision.

The formal economic literature on confirmatory bias provides the closest precedent for the profiling feedback loop as a dynamic process. Rabin and Schrag (1999) model agents who misread disconfirming signals with even small probability q: such agents become locked into false first impressions with positive probability, converging on the wrong hypothesis even with unlimited data. The mechanism -- systematic filtering of disconfirming evidence through the lens of prior belief -- parallels how enforcement decisions filter the evidence available to a profiling agent. Nimark and Sundaresan (2019) show that belief polarization arises even among fully rational Bayesian agents when information costs make selective attention individually rational: agents who attend more to confirming cases and discount information from other contexts exhibit a "complacency effect" that can cause them to stop updating entirely. The profiling agent who concentrates enforcement resources on the profiled group while generating sparse evidence from the rest of the population is behaving as a Nimark-Sundaresan agent -- individually rational, collectively epistemically catastrophic.

Bacchus, Kyburg, and Thalos (1990) provide a principled philosophical basis for the view that formal validity of conditionalization is compatible with epistemic deficiency. They argue that when evidence carries additional structure about its own provenance or the sampling process that generated it, conditioning on the raw proposition can systematically mislead. A profiling agent whose evidence comes only from scrutinized individuals -- a non-representative sample by construction -- is in precisely this position. Meacham (2016) develops a related point through the "error correction" problem: standard conditionalization has no internal mechanism for recognizing and correcting systematically biased evidence histories. An agent who has been updating on biased samples for an extended period has no way, within the standard framework, to identify the distortion or recover from it.

### 2.3 The Applied Epistemology of Profiling -- Connecting Formal and Normative Analysis

The formal results above converge on a diagnosis of the profiling feedback loop as a distinctive kind of epistemic pathology -- neither simple cognitive error nor motivated irrationality, but a structurally induced failure in which formally coherent updating on endogenously generated evidence drives beliefs away from the truth. Several applied-epistemological accounts help to locate and characterize this pathology within a broader landscape.

Lloyd (2021) provides a direct epistemic objection to profiling as a practice. Profiling relies on "naked statistical evidence" -- group-level base rates not causally connected to the individual case -- which can at most justify high credence rather than the outright belief that institutional actions require. Since practices such as stop-and-search typically presuppose outright belief in the suspect's guilt-relevance, the probabilistic evidence base is epistemically insufficient even before the feedback loop is introduced. The research project extends Lloyd's analysis by showing that the statistical base rates themselves are endogenously produced and confounded, undermining even the credence-level justification.

Tversky and Kahneman (1974) demonstrate that human probability judgments systematically neglect base rates in favour of representativeness heuristics -- treating a person as representative of a category and ignoring base-rate heterogeneity within that category. This cognitive tendency may compound the structural pathology: even if an officer were presented with correctly computed conditional probabilities, the representativeness heuristic would incline them toward overweighting the perceived typicality of group membership relative to individual evidence. Van Doorn (2023) nuances this picture, arguing that belief-consistent information processing need not constitute motivated irrationality, because the normative status of differential treatment of confirming versus disconfirming evidence depends on the agent's prior beliefs and the reliability of the evidence. The distinction is important for the profiling case: the problem is not merely that the agent processes evidence in a belief-consistent way, but that the evidence-generating process itself is corrupted by their prior actions -- an underlying structural influence, not a bare differential treatment.

Bovens and Hartmann (2004) establish formally that coherent belief systems can systematically mislead when evidence sources are unreliable or correlated. A profiling agent whose evidence comes from correlated police reports (officers sharing precinct-level expectations) and from the same non-representative sample faces the "unreliable witnesses" problem: adding more coherent but unreliable evidence does not converge toward truth and may actively decrease probability accuracy. Ortoleva (2024) surveys the broader landscape of non-Bayesian updating models, providing a taxonomy that includes confirmatory bias, conservatism, and motivated beliefs -- clarifying that the profiling feedback loop is best understood not as a case of standard motivated reasoning but as a structural instance of the endogenous-evidence pathology that the formal models of Rabin-Schrag and Nimark-Sundaresan characterize.

The combined analysis identifies a precise location for the profiling feedback loop within the Bayesian pathology landscape. It is not a violation of the probability axioms (incoherence); the profiling agent may be fully coherent. It is not primarily base rate neglect in the Tversky-Kahneman sense, though that may accompany it. It is an instance of ecologically irrational but internally coherent updating on endogenously generated, confounded evidence -- a case in which the strongest available justifications for conditionalization (Vassend 2026; Greaves and Wallace 2006) fail together because the evidence-generating process violates their shared presupposition of exogeneity. The research project's contribution is to show formally how the Simpson's paradox mechanism -- poverty as an omitted common cause -- generates the specific confounded statistics on which this epistemically vicious updating operates.

## Section 3: Empirical Grounding and Algorithmic Extensions

The Bayesian feedback model rests on two structural premises: that poverty, not group membership, drives the behaviour being policed; and that the targeted group contains a disproportionately high share of the poor. This section reviews the empirical literature that grounds these premises, then examines how the same feedback mechanism operates at scale in predictive policing systems, where formal impossibility results constrain the available remedies.

### 3.1 Poverty, Race, and the Structural Conditions of the Feedback Loop

The foundational question for the model's empirical premises is whether racial disparities in criminal justice contact reflect differential behaviour or differential enforcement operating on structurally produced poverty. The structural account, developed by Sampson and his collaborators over several decades, holds that neighbourhood-level conditions — concentrated poverty, residential instability, and the erosion of collective efficacy — rather than group-level propensities, are the proximate drivers of elevated crime rates in racially identifiable communities. Sampson, Raudenbush, and Earls (1997) demonstrate this empirically in a multilevel study of 343 Chicago neighbourhoods: after controlling for collective efficacy — social cohesion combined with willingness to intervene on behalf of community norms — the associations between racial composition and violent crime diminish substantially, while concentrated disadvantage and residential instability retain their explanatory force. Sampson (2018) synthesises three decades of this research into a framework of "compounded deprivation," arguing that racial inequality in neighbourhood exposure to poverty operates as a distinct causal layer above and beyond individual socioeconomic status, and that concentrated poverty and racial segregation endure as structural features of American cities reproduced across generations. His 2025 review of frontiers in racial inequality research confirms that the relationship between crime rates and enforcement decisions must be analysed jointly — the crime-enforcement-punishment connection maps directly onto the model's feedback loop — and identifies cumulative structural disadvantage, not individual bias at isolated processing stages, as the primary object of inquiry (Sampson 2025).

The structural premise — that the targeted group contains a disproportionate share of the poor — is documented in detail by Western and Pettit (2010), who show that by 2008 roughly 37 percent of Black male high-school dropouts born between 1975 and 1979 had been imprisoned by their late thirties, establishing incarceration as a normative life event for this specific race-class intersection. Crucially, Western and Pettit also show that standard labour-market statistics undercount disadvantage by excluding incarcerated populations from survey samples, producing spurious improvements in measured Black-white earnings gaps — an illustration of how enforcement-generated selection distorts empirical inference that parallels the model's core mechanism. Western's earlier work (Western 2002) traces one concrete feedback pathway: incarceration substantially reduces subsequent wage growth, and because incarceration is disproportionately prevalent among low-education Black men, the effect of imprisonment on individual wages also deepens aggregate racial wage inequality, reproducing the structural poverty conditions that elevate policing intensity. The result is a cycle — poverty raises enforcement contact, enforcement contact deepens poverty — that is reproduced at the neighbourhood level as well: Western et al. (2021) find that jail incarceration risks in New York City are 40 to 50 percent higher in high-poverty neighbourhoods, with over a quarter of Black men and one sixth of Latino men jailed by age 38.

The political mechanism by which policing resources are allocated differentially to these communities is analysed by Soss and Weaver (2017), who argue that intensive policing in low-income minority communities constitutes a form of governance — the state's "second face" — in which coercion and surveillance substitute for democratic responsiveness. Their concept of "race-class subjugated communities" captures the intersection of racial and economic marginality that determines differential enforcement intensity, mapping onto the model's assumption that targeting is defined by the confluence of poverty and racial minority status rather than either alone. Wacquant (2009) and Garland (2023) situate this pattern within the broader political economy: the expansion of the penal state after the mid-1970s is, on their accounts, a response to the social insecurity generated by deindustrialisation rather than to rising crime, so that the penalisation of poverty is a structural feature of American political economy, not an aberration.

Kurlychek and Johnson (2019) show that the disparities introduced at the initial enforcement stage do not attenuate as cases move through the criminal justice system but compound multiplicatively: each disadvantageous outcome — pretrial detention, prosecution, conviction — increases the probability of a disadvantageous outcome at the next stage, so that racial disparities in policing are amplified, not corrected, downstream. This cumulative disadvantage structure means that enforcement-generated arrest statistics systematically overstate racial differences in criminal behaviour beyond what initial enforcement intensity would predict, a further mechanism by which the statistics used to justify profiling are artefacts of the profiling itself.

What makes this structural account epistemically significant for the model is the point developed by Lee (2024): structural racism operates partly through "perceived neutrality." Statistics generated by racially stratified enforcement appear objective and race-neutral, which is precisely what makes the confounding mechanism — poverty as the omitted causal variable — epistemically serious. When Knox, Lowe, and Mummolo (2020) prove formally that administrative records produced by selective enforcement are non-identified for estimating racial bias, they provide the statistical analogue of Harcourt's (2004) "ratchet effect" argument: the data generated by profiling cannot serve as unbiased evidence for the propensity difference that profiling is supposed to track. Together, these findings ground the model's core structural premise: the poverty-crime nexus, the disproportionate concentration of poverty within racially defined groups, and the enforcement-generated character of the statistics used to justify differential treatment.

### 3.2 Predictive Policing, Fairness Impossibilities, and Feedback at Scale

Predictive policing systems provide the principal contemporary instantiation of the model's feedback mechanism, and the formal literature on these systems demonstrates that the loop operates at scale and is difficult to correct using standard algorithmic interventions.

Ensign et al. (2018) prove formally that predictive policing trained on "discovered crime" data — arrests and stops generated by officer deployment — exhibits runaway feedback driven by Polya urn dynamics: the algorithm concentrates patrol in historically policed areas, generating new arrests there, which feed back into training data and concentrate patrol further. Crucially, this runaway loop occurs even when the initial crime distribution is entirely random, confirming that the self-reinforcing mechanism is a structural property of the training regime rather than merely an artefact of historically biased input data. The distinction Ensign et al. draw between "discovered crime" (endogenous to police behaviour) and victim-reported crime (exogenous to it) maps directly onto the model's distinction between measured and true base rates.

Lum and Isaac (2016) provide the empirical demonstration of this mechanism using the PREDPOL algorithm applied to Oakland drug-arrest data. When PREDPOL is trained on arrest records, its predictions cluster in predominantly Black neighbourhoods even when drug use, estimated from self-reported survey data, is distributed more evenly across the city. The training data reflects historical enforcement patterns rather than the true underlying distribution of the behaviour being policed, and iterative prediction-deployment amplifies the initial disparity. This is the feedback loop the model analyses at the level of individual Bayesian inference: the statistics on which the agent updates are endogenous to the agent's prior enforcement decisions.

Akpinar, De-Arteaga, and Chouldechova (2021) extend this analysis to victim-reported crime data, showing that the bias mechanism is not confined to officer-generated arrests. Differential reporting rates — systematically lower in high-crime, underserved areas — cause hotspot predictions to shift toward moderate-crime, high-reporting neighbourhoods, simultaneously over-policing those areas and under-policing genuinely high-crime zones. The Simpson's paradox structure operates here through a different pathway than in the officer-generated data case: the confounding variable is reporting propensity rather than patrol allocation, but the logical structure — aggregate statistics that reverse the within-stratum association when the confound is included — is the same. This generality supports the project's claim that the Bayesian confounding mechanism is a feature of the broader evidence-generation context rather than a special property of one data source.

The formal impossibility results in algorithmic fairness constrain what remediation of these feedback loops can achieve. Chouldechova (2017) proves that calibration, false-positive parity, and false-negative parity cannot simultaneously be satisfied by any predictor whenever the base rate of the outcome (recidivism, crime) differs across racial groups. Kleinberg, Mullainathan, and Raghavan (2017) prove the same result simultaneously and independently: well-calibrated risk scores cannot satisfy equalised odds unless base rates are equal across groups or the predictor is perfectly accurate. Because predictive policing feedback loops systematically drive base rates upward in over-policed communities, they continuously alter the trade-off frontier, making simultaneous satisfaction of fairness criteria not merely impossible in a static sense but dynamically worsening with each iteration.

The impossibility results have generated a substantial philosophical literature on which criterion should take priority. Hedden (2021) argues that none of the standard statistical criteria — demographic parity, equalised odds, predictive parity — are necessary conditions for fairness, since a manifestly fair and optimal classifier can violate all of them simultaneously; the conclusion that fairness cannot be read off any statistical metric leaves the normative question unresolved by the technical results. Beigang (2023) offers a partial reconciliation by distinguishing predictive fairness (the accuracy of probability estimates) from allocative fairness (the equitable distribution of benefits and burdens), arguing that the impossibility is less sweeping than usually presented because calibration and equalised odds can both be satisfied in the predictive dimension — it is only the allocative use of scores that generates the impossible combination. This distinction is relevant to the project because the feedback loop operates across both dimensions simultaneously: the score is both a predictive claim about recidivism probability and an allocative decision about detention or patrol. Lippert-Rasmussen (2024) challenges the primacy of calibration from a different direction, arguing that analogous calibration requirements are not imposed in non-algorithmic hiring or admissions decisions, and that there is no principled reason to impose a more demanding standard on algorithmic decision-making simply because it quantifies uncertainty explicitly.

Barocas and Selbst (2016) identify the mechanism by which proxy discrimination operates in apparently race-blind systems: when a protected characteristic (race) is correlated with observable proxies (zip code, prior arrests, employment history), a model that includes only the proxies still effectively conditions on race. Hu (2024) deepens this analysis by arguing that race, understood as a social position constituted by a matrix of statistical regularities, cannot be removed from the causal structure of training data by excluding race as an explicit feature, because race is encoded in the causal descendants of racial categorisation — neighbourhood, arrest history, economic variables — that remain in the model. This argument connects directly to the project's concern with omitted variable bias: the attempt to build a race-neutral predictive system that includes causal descendants of race replicates the confounding structure the model analyses, because the race-poverty correlation means that race proxies are themselves correlated with the confound (poverty) that the model's Simpson's paradox analysis identifies as the source of the spurious group-level signal.

Dwork et al. (2012) propose individual fairness — requiring that similar individuals receive similar predictions, where similarity is measured by a task-specific metric — as an alternative to group-level statistical criteria. For the project, Dwork et al.'s observation that group fairness criteria can mask individual discrimination is pertinent: a system can satisfy demographic parity while still systematically misclassifying individuals from historically policed communities, because the group statistics wash out individual injustices. This structural limitation of group-level remedies suggests that correcting the feedback loop requires addressing the confounding mechanism — poverty as the omitted variable — rather than optimising any single fairness metric.

The relationship between individual-level Bayesian updating and group-level algorithmic feedback remains undertheorised in the existing literature. The formal results of Ensign et al. (2018) and Lum and Isaac (2016) operate at the level of iterative training regimes applied to populations; the impossibility theorems of Chouldechova (2017) and Kleinberg, Mullainathan, and Raghavan (2017) are static results about single-round predictors. The project's Bayesian model bridges these levels by identifying Simpson's paradox as the mechanism that operates in the individual updating step and scales up through iteration into the aggregate pattern the algorithmic fairness literature documents. This bridging is the specific analytical contribution the existing literature lacks: the impossibility results take divergent base rates as given, but the project's Simpson's paradox analysis provides a causal diagnosis of how those base-rate disparities are generated and why they are self-reinforcing.

## Conclusion

The three sections of this review converge on a diagnosis that is more precise than either the normative profiling debate or the existing formal literature has produced.

The first finding concerns the epistemic premise on which the philosophical defence of profiling rests. From Risse and Zeckhauser (2004) through Sesardic (2018), the pro-profiling position treats observed crime-rate differentials as independent evidence warranting differential enforcement. The formal economic literature — from Coate and Loury (1993) through Fudenberg and Levine's (1993) self-confirming equilibrium to Esponda and Pouzo's (2016) Berk-Nash equilibrium — establishes that this premise is structurally defeated when enforcement is endogenous: the data-generating process is a function of the beliefs it is supposed to test. Harcourt's (2004) ratchet argument and Knox et al.'s (2020) identification-failure result are the policing-specific instances of this general mechanism.

The second finding concerns the mechanism of corruption. Simpson's paradox, as analysed through Pearl's (2009, 2014) causal-graphical framework, identifies poverty as the omitted confounder — a common cause of both the targeted behaviour and disproportionate group membership among the poor. The Bayesian epistemology literature establishes why this misspecification is self-stabilising: when an agent's evidence stream is endogenous to their prior beliefs and actions, the justifications for conditionalization — Dutch book arguments (Skyrms 1987) or accuracy-dominance arguments (Greaves and Wallace 2006) — do not apply. Vassend (2026) characterises this as ecological irrationality: the agent may be internally coherent while converging away from truth. The combination of Simpson's paradox and endogenous evidence generation produces a loop in which Bayesian updating on confounded aggregate statistics drives beliefs further from the truth, not toward it.

The third finding concerns scale. The structural conditions for the mechanism — concentrated poverty within racially defined communities — are empirically documented by Sampson and Wilson (1995) and the structural-criminology literature. Predictive policing systems instantiate the same loop algorithmically: Ensign et al. (2018) prove that discovered-crime feedback produces runaway patrol concentration regardless of true underlying rates, and the impossibility theorems of Chouldechova (2017) and Kleinberg et al. (2017) establish that no single fairness metric can correct the resulting base-rate disparities without trading off other criteria.

Against this background, the research project's gap is identifiable with precision. No existing work combines a Bayesian model of individual belief updating, Simpson's paradox as the structural confounding mechanism, and the policing-profiling context. The economic models (Coate and Loury 1993; Esponda and Pouzo 2016) lack the specific confounding structure that generates the paradox. The Simpson's paradox literature does not address institutional feedback loops. The Bayesian epistemology literature on endogenous evidence — despite contributions from Vassend (2026), Gallow (2019), and Rabin and Schrag (1999) — has not been brought to bear on discriminatory profiling as an applied case.

Several objections surfaced by the review warrant acknowledgement. Defenders of profiling (Risse and Zeckhauser 2004; Sesardic 2018) may contest the model's empirical premises, arguing that poverty does not fully explain observed differentials and that the assumed causal graph requires independent defence. The formal learning literature offers a partial counter: Fudenberg and Levine (2006) show that false beliefs survive rational learning only when they lie sufficiently far off the equilibrium path, suggesting that institutional design compelling investigation of un-profiled groups could break the self-confirming structure. The algorithmic fairness literature suggests imposed constraints may be more tractable than individual-officer belief revision.

Unresolved questions follow. What conditions on sampling rates, income distributions, and prior strength determine whether Bayesian beliefs converge to or diverge from the truth? Can institutional design — mandatory diversified sampling, inclusion of socioeconomic covariates — break the loop? How does the individual-level Bayesian model connect to the aggregate-level feedback demonstrated by Ensign et al. (2018) in algorithmic systems? These questions define the research programme the model opens rather than closes.
